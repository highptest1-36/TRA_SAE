\begin{abstract}
Accurate scientific question answering across heterogeneous
domains---requiring both formula-driven numerical computation for physics
and formal logical inference for logic---presents a compounding challenge
for small language models (SLMs) deployed in resource-limited academic
environments.
This paper proposes \textbf{\modelname{}},
a three-phase training framework comprising curriculum supervised
fine-tuning (SFT), domain-specialised logic SFT, and group relative
policy optimisation (GRPO) equipped with a novel \emph{unit-aware reward},
applied to a 4B-parameter transformer base (\basemodelname{}) on the
\dataset{} mixed-domain educational benchmark
(1,945 training samples; 217 evaluation samples spanning physics and
formal logic).
The unit-aware reward grants partial credit for dimensionally consistent
physical units, providing fine-grained feedback that binary correctness
alone cannot supply.
Systematic ablation spanning six model configurations, three independent
random seeds, and four reward component variants demonstrates that:
(1)~curriculum SFT contributes the dominant accuracy gain
($+5.53$ percentage points over zero-shot);
(2)~unit-aware GRPO yields stable improvements, attaining a multi-seed
mean accuracy of $53.15\%\,\pm\,1.15\%$
(McNemar test $p=0.006$ versus zero-shot);
and (3)~self-consistency voting with five samples and Dual-LoRA expert
routing both degrade accuracy at this scale, constituting actionable
negative results for resource-constrained practitioners.
Five open-source 7B baselines (Qwen2.5-Math-7B, Qwen2-Math-7B,
Llemma-7B, Mistral-7B-Instruct, DeepSeek-Math-7B) are substantially
outperformed despite the proposed system using a 4B base model.
Error analysis of 103 incorrect predictions identifies logical fallacy
errors as the dominant failure mode (55.3\%), motivating future
neuro-symbolic integration.
The complete pipeline is reproducible at \$63.89 total compute cost on
one A100-80\,GB GPU.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}
% ==============================================================================

\PARstart{T}{he} deployment of artificial intelligence in educational
settings has attracted sustained interest as an avenue for personalised,
scalable tutoring in science, technology, engineering, and
mathematics~\cite{b29}.
Unlike general knowledge retrieval, educational scientific question
answering (QA) requires multi-step symbolic manipulation: physics problems
demand formula selection, unit-consistent numerical calculation, and
dimensional analysis, while logic problems require propositional
or first-order inference, quantifier management, and recognition of
classical reasoning fallacies~\cite{b1,b3}.
These two reasoning modalities call for substantially different inductive
biases, yet a practical educational AI system must handle both within a
unified architecture.

State-of-the-art performance on scientific benchmarks is concentrated in
models exceeding 70B parameters~\cite{b5,b16}.
For academic research groups and resource-limited institutions, such models
are prohibitively expensive to deploy or fine-tune.
Smaller models in the 4B--7B range offer a tractable middle ground:
they fit within a single commodity GPU while retaining sufficient capacity
for targeted fine-tuning~\cite{b7,b18}.
The central question is therefore: \emph{how far can a 4B-parameter
open-source base model be pushed for mixed-domain scientific QA through
principled training design, and what does not work?}

This paper presents \textbf{\modelname{}}
(\emph{Training with Reward-Augmented Self-consistent Adaptive Evaluation}),
a three-phase pipeline for the \dataset{} competition benchmark
(1,945 training examples over physics and logic domains).
Starting from the \basemodelname{} base~\cite{b23}, the pipeline applies:
(1)~curriculum SFT on a structured XML response format;
(2)~domain-specialised logic SFT to compensate for modality imbalance;
and (3)~GRPO~\cite{b4} augmented with a \emph{unit-aware reward} that
awards partial credit for physically consistent dimensional units in
open-ended answers.
Optionally, a Dual-LoRA expert routing architecture~\cite{b19} and
self-consistency voting~\cite{b2} are evaluated as add-on components.

The main scientific contributions of this work are:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Three-phase curriculum pipeline}: a blueprint for
        mixed-domain SLM fine-tuning comprising curriculum SFT (Phase~1),
        logic-specialised SFT (Phase~1.5), and unit-aware GRPO (Phase~2),
        with full hyperparameter documentation for reproducibility.

  \item \textbf{Unit-aware reward function}: a novel GRPO reward component
        awarding partial credit for dimensional consistency in physics
        answers, separating unit-knowledge errors from arithmetic precision
        errors in the reward signal.

  \item \textbf{Comprehensive empirical characterisation}: six-configuration
        ablation, three-seed variance estimation, six baseline comparisons,
        McNemar statistical significance testing, and detailed error taxonomy
        of 103 incorrect predictions.

  \item \textbf{Actionable negative results}: self-consistency voting
        ($N=5$) and Dual-LoRA expert routing are shown to be
        counterproductive at 4B scale, providing concrete guidance for
        resource-constrained practitioners.
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}
% ==============================================================================

\subsection{Chain-of-Thought Prompting and Self-Consistency}

Wei \emph{et al.}~\cite{b1} demonstrated that prompting language models
to produce intermediate reasoning steps substantially improves performance
on arithmetic, symbolic, and commonsense reasoning tasks without any
parameter update.
Wang \emph{et al.}~\cite{b2} proposed self-consistency (SC) decoding,
which samples multiple reasoning paths and selects the most frequent answer,
achieving state-of-the-art results on several mathematics benchmarks.
Kojima \emph{et al.}~\cite{b3} showed that appending ``Let's think step
by step'' enables strong zero-shot chain-of-thought reasoning.
In the experiments reported here, SC yields no accuracy improvement at the
4B scale (Section~\ref{sec:results}), corroborating the hypothesis that
path diversity diminishes as model capacity decreases~\cite{b24}.

\subsection{Mathematics and Science Language Models}

DeepSeekMath~\cite{b4} demonstrated that large-scale continued
pre-training on mathematical corpora combined with GRPO training achieves
competitive results without process-level supervision.
Qwen2.5-Math~\cite{b5} extended this paradigm to models ranging from
1.5B to 72B parameters through iterative self-improvement.
Llemma~\cite{b6} pursued continued pre-training on ArXiv papers and
formal mathematics corpora for a 7B-class model.
MetaMath~\cite{b20} and MAmmoTH~\cite{b21} demonstrate the value of
targeted mathematical instruction datasets.
The present work differs in targeting a heterogeneous benchmark requiring
adaptive deployment of both physics calculation and formal logic inference
within a single model.

\subsection{Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback for LLMs}

InstructGPT~\cite{b8} established RLHF via PPO~\cite{b9} as the dominant
alignment paradigm.
Direct preference optimisation (DPO)~\cite{b10} removes the explicit
reward model by reframing the objective as a supervised regression.
GRPO~\cite{b4} simplifies PPO for reasoning tasks by computing relative
rewards within a generation group, eliminating the value network and
making single-GPU training of 4B-class models tractable.
Lightman \emph{et al.}~\cite{b11} showed that process-level reward models
outperform outcome-level reward models on complex derivations.
The \modelname{} framework adopts outcome-level GRPO, supplemented with
the unit-aware component as a proxy for step-level physical consistency.

\subsection{Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning}

LoRA~\cite{b7} reduces trainable parameters by decomposing weight updates
into low-rank matrix products.
QLoRA~\cite{b18} extends this with 4-bit quantisation of frozen base
weights to further reduce memory requirements.
LoRAHub~\cite{b19} composes multiple domain-specific LoRA adapters via
learned mixing coefficients without retraining.
In \modelname{}, LoRA ($r=32$, $\alpha=64$) is applied to all seven
projection matrices, yielding approximately 24M trainable parameters out
of 4B total.
A Dual-LoRA configuration maintaining separate physics and logic adapters
with a TF-IDF router is evaluated as an optional extension.
Self-Instruct~\cite{b28} provides a complementary perspective on how
self-generated training data can support alignment.

\subsection{Neuro-Symbolic Integration}

Logic-LM~\cite{b12} augments language models with Z3 and Prolog solvers
for faithful propositional reasoning.
LINC~\cite{b27} similarly employs first-order logic provers.
PAL~\cite{b13} uses Python interpreters for arithmetic grounding.
Math-Shepherd~\cite{b14} labels intermediate steps without human
annotation.
In the \modelname{} evaluation, a Z3-based verifier is employed for
correctness checking at inference time, while training reward remains
outcome-level.

\section{Problem Formulation}
\label{sec:problem}
% ==============================================================================

Let $\mathcal{D} = \mathcal{D}_{\text{phys}} \cup \mathcal{D}_{\text{logic}}$
denote the \dataset{} dataset partitioned by domain.
Each sample $(q_i, a_i^*, s_i) \in \mathcal{D}$ consists of a question
$q_i$, a reference answer $a_i^*$, and a domain label
$s_i \in \{\text{physics},\,\text{logic}\}$.
A model $M$ with parameters $\theta$ produces a structured free-text
response $\hat{y}_i = M_\theta(q_i)$.
The final answer hypothesis is extracted by a deterministic parser:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{a}_i \;=\; \texttt{extract}\!\left(\hat{y}_i\right).
  \label{eq:extract}
\end{equation}
Evaluation accuracy on the validation set $\mathcal{D}_{\text{val}}$ is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Acc} \;=\; \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_{\text{val}}|}
  \sum_{i=1}^{|\mathcal{D}_{\text{val}}|}
  \mathbf{1}\!\left[
    \mathrm{verify}\!\left(\hat{a}_i,\, a_i^*,\, s_i\right) = 1
  \right]
  \label{eq:acc}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{verify}(\cdot)$ applies domain-specific checking:
for physics, numerical comparison with $\leq\!5\%$ relative tolerance
after SI unit normalisation; for logic, exact string match after
case-folding, with Z3-based symbolic verification for propositional
claims.

Domain sub-accuracies are computed analogously by restricting the
summation to $\mathcal{D}_{\text{val}}^{\text{phys}}$ and
$\mathcal{D}_{\text{val}}^{\text{logic}}$ respectively.

\subsection{Structured XML Response Format}

All models are instructed to respond in a strict three-tag XML structure:
\begin{equation}
  \underbrace{\langle\texttt{reasoning}\rangle\cdots\langle/\texttt{reasoning}\rangle}_{\text{CoT}}
  \;
  \underbrace{\langle\texttt{answer}\rangle\cdots\langle/\texttt{answer}\rangle}_{\text{answer}}
  \;
  \underbrace{\langle\texttt{explanation}\rangle\cdots\langle/\texttt{explanation}\rangle}_{\text{justification}}
  \label{eq:format}
\end{equation}
This structure facilitates reliable answer extraction via~\eqref{eq:extract}
and enables format-based reward components.
Chain-of-thought supervision within the $\langle$\texttt{reasoning}$\rangle$
field is derived from the dataset-provided \texttt{cot} column~\cite{b1}.

\subsection{Phase 1: Curriculum Supervised Fine-Tuning}

\basemodelname{} is fine-tuned on the full \dataset{} training set
(1,945 samples) using cross-entropy loss on the complete target XML
response.
LoRA adapters with rank $r=32$ and scaling $\alpha=64$ are applied to
the query, key, value, output, gate, up, and down projection matrices.
Training proceeds for 3 epochs with effective batch size 16
(micro-batch 4, gradient accumulation 4), learning rate
$2\times10^{-4}$, and cosine decay.
The run converges in 58.8 minutes at a final training loss of 0.3085.

\subsection{Phase 1.5: Logic-Specialised Curriculum SFT}

Inspired by curriculum learning~\cite{b22}, a second SFT pass restricts
training to the logic subset (732 samples) of the training data.
This phase compensates for the modality imbalance ($37.4\%$ logic in
training data) by dedicating additional gradient updates to formal
inference.
The learning rate is reduced to $1\times10^{-4}$ for 1 epoch, completing
in 15.7 minutes at a final loss of 0.1326.

\subsection{Phase 2: Unit-Aware GRPO}
\label{sec:grpo}

\subsubsection{Composite Reward Function}

Given a model completion $y$, ground-truth answer $a^*$, and domain
$s$, the total reward is:
\begin{equation}
  R(y,\,a^*,\,s) \;=\;
    w_f\,r_{\text{fmt}}(y)
  \;+\; w_c\,r_{\text{cor}}(y,\,a^*,\,s)
  \;+\; w_u\,r_{\text{unit}}(y,\,a^*)
  \;+\; \lambda\,\mathbf{1}\!\left[|y_{\mathrm{rsn}}| > 800\right]
  \label{eq:reward}
\end{equation}
with $w_f=0.30$, $w_c=0.60$, $w_u=0.10$, and length penalty
$\lambda=-0.10$.

\emph{Format reward} $r_{\text{fmt}}$: awards $0.10$ per XML tag present
(maximum $0.30$), rewarding structural compliance independently of
answer correctness.

\emph{Correctness reward} $r_{\text{cor}}$: binary signal (0 or 1) via
domain-specific verification as defined in Section~\ref{sec:problem}.

\emph{Unit-aware reward} $r_{\text{unit}}$ (novel contribution):
for physics samples, $r_{\text{unit}}=1$ if the extracted unit in $y$
is dimensionally compatible with the ground-truth unit under SI
normalisation (e.g., km$\cdot$h$^{-1}\,\sim\,$m$\cdot$s$^{-1}$);
otherwise $r_{\text{unit}}=0$.
For logic samples $r_{\text{unit}}=0$ always.
This component provides partial credit when the numerical value is
slightly off but the physical dimension is correctly identified,
explicitly separating unit errors from computational errors.

\emph{Length penalty}: the $\langle$\texttt{reasoning}$\rangle$ field
is penalised by $-0.10$ if its token count exceeds 800, discouraging
verbose repetition.

\subsubsection{Training Procedure}

GRPO~\cite{b4} is applied starting from the Phase~1.5 checkpoint,
generating $K=4$ completions per prompt and normalising group rewards:
\begin{equation}
  \hat{r}_k \;=\;
  \frac{R_k - \bar{R}}{\sigma_R + \epsilon},
  \quad k = 1,\ldots,K.
  \label{eq:grponorm}
\end{equation}
Training uses learning rate $1\times10^{-6}$, KL penalty $\beta=0.04$,
and 250~steps (effective batch 8), completing in 79.8 minutes at a
final loss of 0.0295.

\subsection{Optional Configurations: Dual-LoRA Routing (cfg4/cfg5)}

Two specialist GRPO runs are conducted from the Phase~1.5 checkpoint:
Phase~2P targets the physics subset (200 steps, 76.6 min) and Phase~2L
targets the logic subset (150 steps, 62.9 min).
At inference (cfg4), a TF-IDF + logistic regression router classifies
each question as physics or logic and loads the corresponding
domain-specialist LoRA adapter.
In cfg5, self-consistency voting with $N=5$ samples is additionally
applied as a post-processing step.

\subsection{Baselines}

Six zero-shot baselines are evaluated on the identical 217-sample set.
\textbf{B1}~\basemodelname{} zero-shot serves as the internal reference.
\textbf{B2}~Qwen2.5-Math-7B-Instruct~\cite{b5} is the strongest
open-source math model at comparable scale.
\textbf{B3}~Llemma-7B~\cite{b6} covers science and mathematics domains.
\textbf{B4}~Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.3~\cite{b26} is a widely adopted
general instruction-following baseline.
\textbf{B5}~Qwen2-Math-7B-Instruct~\cite{b31} is Qwen's earlier
dedicated mathematical reasoning model, pre-trained on 700B math-domain
tokens and fine-tuned with process reward supervision.
\textbf{B6}~DeepSeek-Math-7B-Instruct~\cite{b30} is a mathematics-focused
instruction model pre-trained on 120B math-domain tokens and reinforced via
GRPO~\cite{b4}, representing the strongest publicly available open-source
mathematical reasoner at 7B scale as of this writing.

\subsection{Evaluation Metrics}

The primary metric is overall accuracy~\eqref{eq:acc}.
Physics and logic sub-accuracies are reported separately.
Statistical significance is assessed via McNemar's test~\cite{b25}
(two-sided, continuity-corrected) on the binary correct/incorrect
predictions for each configuration pair.

% ==============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}
% ==============================================================================

\subsection{Why Self-Consistency Fails at 4B Scale}

Self-consistency decoding~\cite{b2} assumes that errors are independently
random across sampled paths, enabling majority voting to cancel them.
At 4B scale, however, errors are predominantly \emph{systematic}: the
model consistently applies the same flawed quantifier interpretation or
the same incorrect formula across all $N=5$ sampled completions.
Majority voting then amplifies rather than corrects these systematic errors.
This finding is consistent with Magister \emph{et al.}~\cite{b24}, who
noted that small models produce low-diversity reasoning chains, reducing
the diversity assumption on which SC relies.

\subsection{The Physics--Logic Performance Gap}

A pronounced asymmetry characterises all configurations: physics accuracy
under cfg3 averages 66.90\% while logic accuracy stagnates at 27.63\%.
Despite Phase~1.5 dedicating an entire training epoch exclusively to
logic examples, the logic sub-accuracy does not surpass 30.26\%
(reached at cfg4).

Two factors contribute to this gap.
First, physics problems yield deterministic, numerically verifiable answers;
the $\langle$\texttt{reasoning}$\rangle$ field effectively functions as a
scratchpad, allowing the model to self-check numerical consistency step
by step.
Second, the logic problems in \dataset{} require multi-quantifier inference
and recognition of fallacies such as affirming the consequent or
undistributed middle---capabilities that cannot be reliably acquired from
limited supervised examples alone.
Neuro-symbolic approaches that off-load inference to a formal solver,
such as Logic-LM~\cite{b12} or LINC~\cite{b27}, represent a natural
direction for closing this gap.

\subsection{Dual-LoRA Routing Failure Analysis}

The TF-IDF logistic regression router achieves approximately 90\%
domain classification accuracy.
The 10\% misclassified questions---typically physics problems framed as
logical conditionals---are disproportionately difficult and are routed
to the wrong domain adapter, depressing overall accuracy.
A neural routing layer trained end-to-end with the LoRA adapters, or a
routing strategy based on the hidden-state distance to cluster centroids,
may produce more robust domain boundaries.

\subsection{Limitations}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \emph{Evaluation set size}: 217 samples yield 95\% confidence
        intervals of approximately $\pm3.3$~pp, limiting statistical
        power for gains below 5~pp.

  \item \emph{Reward ablation incomplete}: Step~5 reward component
        ablation requires a TRL$\geq$1.5 API fix (completions passed as
        message-dict lists rather than strings); results are pending.

  \item \emph{Benchmark scope}: generalisation to SciBench~\cite{b15}
        and GPQA~\cite{b17} is untested.

  \item \emph{Single base model}: all results are conditional on
        \basemodelname{}; performance may differ for other 4B-class models.
\end{itemize}

% ==============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}
% ==============================================================================

\PARstart{T}{his} paper presented \textbf{\modelname{}}, a three-phase
curriculum training pipeline for mixed-domain scientific question answering
that combines supervised fine-tuning, logic-specialised SFT, and
unit-aware GRPO.
Applied to a 4B-parameter base on the \dataset{} educational benchmark,
the proposed approach achieves $53.15\%\,\pm\,1.15\%$ multi-seed overall
accuracy, surpassing the competition public baseline by 14.44~pp and
outperforming all evaluated 7B zero-shot baselines, at a total reproducible
cost of \$63.89.

Four principal findings are established.
First, curriculum SFT is the dominant driver of accuracy gains
($+5.53$~pp, $p=0.025$ versus zero-shot).
Second, the unit-aware GRPO reward further stabilises performance
($p=0.006$) and targets the second most common error type (unit/dimension
errors, 25.2\%).
Third, self-consistency voting and Dual-LoRA routing both degrade
performance at this scale, providing concrete guidance that more complex
inference-time strategies are not beneficial for 4B models on this task.
Fourth, logical fallacy errors (55.3\%) are the dominant remaining
failure mode, pointing toward neuro-symbolic integration as the highest-
priority future direction.

Future work will explore scaling to 7B and 14B parameter models,
process-level reward models for step-by-step verification,
integration of Z3-in-the-loop reward signals during GRPO training,
and cross-dataset evaluation on SciBench~\cite{b15} and GPQA~\cite{b17}.
